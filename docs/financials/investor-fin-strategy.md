# Investor Financing Strategy

## Purpose

This document defines how CLARK should approach investor capital as a structured financing strategy rather than as a generic fundraising exercise.

It is meant to work alongside:

- `docs/financials/investors-and-investment-firms.md`
- `docs/process/investor-selection-criteria.md`

- `docs/research/supporting/clark_buffalo_rochester_market_research.md`

Verification date for external investor scan in this memo: `2026-03-28`

## Core Rule

CLARK should not try to raise money for a vague industrial platform.

CLARK should raise capital against a specific proof sequence:

1. Hamilton training revenue proof
2. Hamilton site activation proof at `24 Clark Ave`
3. Niagara Clarkware proof
4. first paid partner-facility economics proof

That means investor targeting must start with people who can understand mixed software, manufacturing, training, and corridor-building risk.

## What CLARK Is Actually Raising For

The near-term financing ask should support:

- Hamilton training launch and operating runway
- Clark Courses launch
- Clarkware pilot development and deployment
- first partner-facility qualification and onboarding work
- selective co-location activation at `24 Clark Ave`

This is not yet a pure software raise.

It is not yet a traditional manufacturing plant raise either.

It is a proof-capital raise for a training-first industrial platform with a software wedge and a corridor thesis.

## Strategic Investor Lanes

CLARK should run four parallel investor lanes.

### Lane 1: Ontario / Canada industrial and deep-tech investors

Best for:

- Clarkware
- facility software
- industrial workflow
- advanced-manufacturing platform narrative

### Lane 2: Buffalo / New York public-capital and accelerator investors

Best for:

- Buffalo-side entity build
- relocation-linked capital
- matching capital
- early U.S. ecosystem credibility

### Lane 3: Strategic operator and corridor stakeholders

Best for:

- intros
- pilot environments
- anchor credibility
- nontraditional capital or special-purpose participation

### Lane 4: Lenders, guarantees, and non-dilutive finance

Best for:

- equipment
- leaseholds
- working capital
- tax-credit recovery

This lane is covered separately in `docs/financials/canadian-fin-programs.md` and should run in parallel with equity work.

## First-Round Investor Shape

The strongest near-term CLARK investor is likely to have most of these traits:

- comfortable with industrial software, advanced manufacturing, or deep tech
- willing to fund a company before the software story is fully standalone
- tolerant of hardware-adjacent and facility-adjacent complexity
- interested in real-economy, corridor, or manufacturing productivity stories
- able to help with follow-on financing once CLARK has proof

The strongest near-term round structure is likely:

- one or two thesis-fit lead or anchor investors
- one public or quasi-public matching or accelerator source if Buffalo structure becomes active
- a small set of strategic angels, operators, or family-office style backers with manufacturing or regional stakes

## What CLARK Should Not Do

- Do not start with generic software seed funds that only want pure SaaS.
- Do not rely only on Buffalo public capital before CLARK has a real New York operating structure.
- Do not over-index on strategics who could become channel conflict or information-risk problems.
- Do not treat every wealthy operator as a fit just because they are in manufacturing.

## Support Structure For Ongoing Investor Work

CLARK should use four separate support documents:

- `docs/financials/investors-and-investment-firms.md`
  - for maintaining the live master list of names to track
- `docs/process/investor-selection-criteria.md`
  - for evaluating a specific investor once identified
