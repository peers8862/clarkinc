# CC-L1-01 Instructor Guide

**Course:** The Electronics Manufacturing Industry: History, Scale, and Structure
**Level:** Clark Courses Level 1 Foundations
**Recommended Delivery Length:** 4 hours
**Recommended Cohort Size:** 8 to 16 participants
**Primary Use:** Internal CLARK delivery, partner-facility orientation cohorts, workforce onboarding sessions

---

## Purpose

This guide turns the core course brief into a teachable session. It is written for an instructor who is technically literate and professionally credible, but who may not have spent years inside every segment of the electronics manufacturing industry. The goal is not to perform expertise theatrically. The goal is to help participants build a usable mental model of the industry they are entering.

The single most important outcome is that participants stop thinking about electronics manufacturing as "a factory that builds boards" and start seeing it as a coordinated system of design owners, material suppliers, board fabricators, assemblers, testers, regulators, and end-market requirements.

---

## Delivery Objectives

By the end of the session, participants should be able to:

- explain the historical shift from hand-wired assemblies to through-hole and then to SMT
- distinguish OEM, ODM, EMS, and CM models in plain language
- describe the basic upstream and downstream nodes around PCB assembly
- explain why the 2020 to 2022 shortage changed supply-chain behavior
- discuss why some electronics work is returning to North America and why some is not
- place CLARK within the current electronics manufacturing landscape

## Room Setup

- classroom or conference layout
- participants seated in pairs or small groups of 3 to 4
- projector visible from all seats
- wall or board space for timeline and ecosystem mapping

Avoid a pure lecture layout if possible. This course works best when participants can turn and compare mental models with each other.

## Session Plan

## Block 1: Opening and Orientation

**Time:** 20 minutes

### Instructor goals

- establish that the course is about industry structure, not memorizing trivia
- surface what the room already believes about electronics manufacturing
- give participants a reason to care

### Run of show

1. Welcome participants and explain the purpose of the course.
2. Ask each participant to say their role or intended role and one thing they think "electronics manufacturing" means.
3. Write recurring words on the board: factory, chips, Asia, boards, supply chain, quality, automation.
4. Explain that the course will refine and in some cases overturn those first assumptions.

### Suggested opening script

"Most people enter electronics manufacturing through one narrow door. They know soldering, or procurement, or quality, or product design, but they do not yet see the full system. This session is about building that system view."

---

## Block 2: From Chassis Wiring to PCB Standardization

**Time:** 35 minutes

### Core points

- before PCBs, electronics was labor-intensive and hard to scale
- the PCB was a manufacturing system shift, not a minor convenience
- military adoption accelerated standardization and reliability expectations

### Teaching moves

- compare point-to-point wiring with a modern assembled board
- draw a simple before-and-after diagram on the board
- ask what becomes easier when connections are designed into the substrate

### Check for understanding

Ask: "Why does a printed circuit board make scale possible in a way hand wiring does not?"

Strong answer themes:

- repeatability
- lower labor per unit
- easier inspection
- denser packaging
- more consistent electrical paths

---

## Block 3: Through-Hole to SMT and the Economics of Scale

**Time:** 40 minutes

### Core points

- through-hole remains useful but SMT changed density, speed, and cost structure
- SMT favored automation and capital investment
- that capital intensity helped create the EMS model

### Teaching moves

- explain the basic SMT sequence in one minute: paste, place, reflow, inspect
- contrast mechanical robustness of through-hole with density and speed of SMT
- connect packaging change directly to business-model change

**Time:** 10 minutes

## Block 4: OEM, ODM, EMS, CM, and the Rest of the Ecosystem

**Time:** 45 minutes

### Core points

- OEM owns the brand and product responsibility
- ODM designs and manufactures a white-labelable product
- EMS builds to another company's design
- CM is often used broadly, but EMS usually implies broader service scope
- fabrication, distribution, test, and design support are distinct nodes

### Exercise

Run the company-sorting activity from the course deck. Let groups sort the provided company list, then debrief ambiguous cases.

---

## Block 5: The 2020 to 2022 Shortage and What It Changed

**Time:** 35 minutes

### Core points

- the shortage was a stack of shocks, not one isolated failure
- semiconductor concentration and JIT fragility were exposed
- post-shortage behavior changed in purchasing, inventory, and design

### Exercise

Use the chip-shortage timeline worksheet from the participant workbook. Have pairs identify:

- earliest plausible intervention point
- one procurement action
- one structural industry change

---

## Block 6: Reshoring, Near-Shoring, and the North American Opportunity

**Time:** 30 minutes

### Core points

- not all electronics work is returning
- high-mix, regulated, quality-sensitive work is more likely to regionalize
- resilience, lead time, and control matter as much as labor rate in many segments

---

## Block 7: Where CLARK Fits

**Time:** 20 minutes

### Core points

- CLARK is framed as a node-network operator, not just a single-site assembler
- workforce quality is treated as strategic infrastructure
- Clark Courses exists because capability and judgment are bottlenecks

---

## Block 8: Assessment and Close

**Time:** 25 minutes

### Run of show

1. Give the short quiz.
2. Review answers quickly in the room.
3. Ask participants to write one sentence completing this prompt: "The part of the industry I understand differently now is..."
4. Preview the next course.

---

## Suggested Assessment Standard

Participants have met the course standard if they can:

- correctly classify core business models
- explain the broad historical sequence
- identify at least four ecosystem nodes around assembly
- articulate at least two lasting post-shortage changes
- describe CLARK's position without confusing it with a generic tier-1 EMS model
