# The Electronics Manufacturing Industry
## History, Scale, and Structure

**Clark Courses**
Level 1 Foundations

Hamilton, Ontario  
CLARK

## Why This Course Exists

- Most people enter electronics manufacturing through one narrow role
- The industry is larger and more interconnected than it first appears
- Better context improves communication, judgment, and quality

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you should be able to:

- describe the major phases of industry development
- explain `OEM`, `ODM`, `EMS`, and `CM`
- identify the main nodes around PCB assembly
- explain the 2020-2022 chip shortage at a high level
- describe reshoring and near-shoring logic
- place CLARK within the industry landscape

## Starting Question

When you hear **electronics manufacturing**, what comes to mind?

- factory
- chips
- boards
- Asia
- automation
- quality
- supply chain

## Before the PCB

Before printed circuit boards:

- electronics were often hand-wired
- components were mounted to chassis
- wiring was manual and labor-intensive
- scaling production was difficult

## What the PCB Changed

The PCB made electronics manufacturing:

- more repeatable
- more compact
- easier to document
- easier to inspect
- easier to scale

## Early Industry Development

1940s to 1960s:

- wartime demand accelerated development
- printed boards moved into broader commercial use
- plated-through-hole construction became more common
- reliability expectations began to formalize

## The Through-Hole Era

1960s to early 1980s:

- components inserted through drilled holes
- strong mechanical retention
- hand assembly and wave soldering were common
- still relevant today in selected applications

## Surface Mount Technology

SMT changed the industry by enabling:

- smaller components
- denser layouts
- double-sided assembly
- faster automated placement
- higher throughput

## Why SMT Changed Business Structure

SMT favored firms that could invest in:

- pick-and-place equipment
- reflow ovens
- inspection systems
- process engineering
- high utilization across multiple customers

## Core Business Models

- `OEM`: owns the product and brand
- `ODM`: designs and manufactures a product others can sell
- `EMS`: manufactures to a customer's design
- `CM`: broad term for contract manufacturing

## The Ecosystem Around Assembly

A finished product depends on more than the assembler:

- component manufacturers
- component distributors
- PCB fabricators
- design houses
- EMS providers
- test resources
- logistics and end customers

## The China Manufacturing Decade

1990s to mid-2010s:

- major shift toward China-based manufacturing
- labor cost advantages
- dense supplier ecosystems
- strong infrastructure and scale
- major cost and speed benefits

## Trade-Offs of Offshore Scale

<!-- _class: grid-2 -->

### Advantages

- lower unit cost
- large supplier base
- scale
- speed

### Trade-offs

- longer supply chains
- logistics risk
- quality-management distance
- IP and control concerns

## The 2020-2022 Shock

The shortage came from multiple shocks at once:

- pandemic-related shutdowns
- sudden electronics demand surge
- automotive order cancellations and rebound
- concentrated semiconductor capacity
- long lead times and spot-market distortion

## What Changed After the Shortage

Many manufacturers increased focus on:

- buffer inventory
- long-term agreements
- alternate approvals
- design for component availability
- reducing single-source exposure

## Geopolitics and Industrial Policy

Key issues:

- concentration of advanced fabrication in Taiwan
- semiconductor sovereignty concerns
- CHIPS Act and similar national programs
- regional manufacturing incentives

## Reshoring and Near-Shoring

- **Reshoring**: bringing work back to the home country
- **Near-shoring**: moving work to a nearby country

For North America, near-shoring often means **Mexico**.

## Not All Work Reshores

<!-- _class: grid-2 -->

### More likely to regionalize

- high-mix, low-volume work
- regulated products
- quality-sensitive assemblies
- products needing responsiveness

### Less likely to regionalize

- ultra-high-volume commodity consumer products
- products optimized mainly for lowest unit cost
- products dependent on massive supplier clustering

## North American EMS Landscape

The region includes:

- global tier-1 players
- regional specialists
- niche high-reliability firms
- medical, industrial, aerospace, telecom, and defense suppliers

## Vertical Markets Matter

Different markets create different demands:

- consumer
- industrial
- medical
- aerospace and defense
- automotive
- telecom and infrastructure

Each has its own quality, volume, and regulatory profile.

## Key Takeaways

- Electronics manufacturing is a system
- History shaped today’s business structure
- Supply chain design is strategic
- Not every product follows the same logic
- Workforce capability matters
