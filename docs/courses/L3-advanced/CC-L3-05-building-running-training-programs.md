# CC-L3-05: Building and Running a Training Program — Curriculum Delivery, Instructor Development, and Clark Partnership

**Clark Courses — Level 3: Advanced Topics**
**Facility:** Clark, Hamilton, Ontario
**Course Code:** CC-L3-05

---

## 1. Course Overview

Teaching electronics manufacturing well is a skill that can be developed systematically, and this course is designed to help people develop it. It is aimed at those who will deliver Clark Courses, build complementary training programs within their organizations, or lead internal training functions at electronics manufacturers. The course covers the principles of adult learning that govern how manufacturing professionals absorb and apply new knowledge, the mechanics of curriculum delivery in technical subjects, and the preparation practices that allow an instructor to deliver with confidence on content they have not personally practiced on the shop floor. The course also addresses the Clark partnership model — how Clark Courses are licensed, co-delivered, and quality-assured, and how the broader Clark ecosystem of the Hamilton facility, the IPE platform, and IPC certification pathways connects to the training program. Participants leave with a practical framework for planning, delivering, and improving technical training sessions, and with the confidence to engage with course material as professional instructors rather than subject-matter experts performing lectures.

---

## 2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, participants will be able to:

- Apply the key principles of adult learning (andragogy) to the design and delivery of electronics manufacturing training, including connecting content to real work problems, managing mixed-experience cohorts, and building intrinsic motivation
- Write learning objectives at multiple levels of Bloom's taxonomy and explain why the distinction between awareness, skills, and judgment training changes how a session must be designed and delivered
- Structure a training session — including agenda design, warm-up, content delivery, discussion facilitation, consolidation, and close — and apply time-management techniques to recover from running behind schedule
- Prepare to instruct on technical content that they have not personally practiced by reading primary sources, interviewing practitioners, and building a robust question set that allows them to engage questions at the boundary of their knowledge
- Design and conduct formative and summative assessments for technical adult learners, and deliver constructive feedback that improves performance without diminishing engagement
- Explain the Clark partnership model: how Clark Courses are licensed, the train-the-trainer pathway, the curriculum licensing structure for in-house delivery, and the quality standards that govern licensed delivery
- Describe how Clark Courses connects to the Clark Hamilton facility, the Clark IPE platform, and IPC certification pathways, and explain how each element of the Clark ecosystem supports the educational mission

## 3. Target Audience

This course is designed for current or prospective Clark Courses instructors, internal training leads at electronics manufacturing companies who will deliver Clark Courses under license, quality managers and engineers who conduct in-house training as part of their quality system obligations, and senior manufacturing professionals who are transitioning from individual contributor to trainer or coach roles. It is also relevant for anyone who is evaluating whether to become a Clark training partner or who is responsible for managing a training program that includes Clark Courses components.

## 4. Prerequisites

**Recommended prior courses:**

- Completion of at least one Clark Courses Level 1 and one Clark Courses Level 2 course as a participant (to have experienced the curriculum from the learner's perspective)
- No formal education background is required

Participants should have sufficient industry experience to draw on real-world examples from electronics manufacturing in their delivery. A minimum of three to five years in a manufacturing, engineering, or quality role is typical for this audience, but the course is designed to be accessible to motivated participants with less experience who are entering training roles.

---

## 5. Duration and Format

**Duration:** 1.5 days (approximately 10-11 instructional hours)

**Format:** Instructor-led classroom with significant participant practice time. This course is itself a model of the delivery methods it teaches — the structure, pacing, discussion facilitation, and assessment practices used during CC-L3-05 are intentionally aligned with the principles being taught. Participants should expect to give two short practice deliveries (five to ten minutes each) on assigned technical topics, receive structured peer and instructor feedback, and participate actively in discussion-based learning. The course is not a lecture — it is experiential.

## 6. Module Outline

### Module 1: How Adults Learn — Principles and Implications for Manufacturing Training

**Session 1.1: Andragogy — The Adult Learning Framework**

Malcolm Knowles' concept of andragogy (adult learning) is the foundation of effective professional training and is distinct in important ways from the pedagogy of school-based learning. The six principles of andragogy and their implications for electronics manufacturing training are explored in this session: adults need to know why they are learning something before they invest effort (implication: begin every session by connecting the content to a real work problem, not by explaining the agenda); adults bring experience that is a resource, not a deficit (implication: draw on cohort experience, do not treat the room as empty vessels); adults' readiness to learn is tied to real-life application (implication: abstract theory without practical grounding loses adult learners quickly); adults are problem-oriented rather than subject-oriented (implication: organize content around problems and tasks, not topics and chapters); adults are internally motivated (implication: the instructor's job is to connect the content to intrinsic motivation, not to motivate through external pressure); adults need to feel respected and treated as capable (implication: never condescend, never embarrass, always acknowledge what participants already know). The session closes with a discussion of what this means specifically for the shop floor population: experienced operators who have been doing a job for ten years often need to be shown why new knowledge is better than what they have, not just told that it is.

**Session 1.2: Mixed-Experience Cohorts and Intrinsic Motivation**

Real training cohorts in electronics manufacturing are rarely homogeneous. A session on IPC-A-610 Class 3 criteria might include a ten-year inspector, a new graduate engineer, an operations manager with no soldering background, and a customer quality representative. This session addresses the challenge: how do you design and deliver for a room where one person finds the content trivially familiar and another finds it overwhelmingly new? Techniques covered include: pre-session audience calibration (a brief, non-threatening warm-up question that surfaces the range of experience in the room), tiered examples (illustrate every concept at a basic level first, then at a more complex level for those ready for it), the use of experienced participants as teaching resources (inviting practitioners to share their experience rather than performing at them), and differentiated exercises (exercises where participants can go deep or stay at the surface and both options are productive). The session also addresses intrinsic motivation: most people in manufacturing roles are motivated by competence and by producing good work — connecting training content to that motivation ("this will make you better at the job you already care about") is more effective than any external incentive.

### Module 2: Curriculum Design — Objectives, Taxonomy, and Session Architecture

**Session 2.1: Learning Objectives and Bloom's Taxonomy**

A learning objective is a statement of what a participant will be able to do at the end of a learning experience — not what the instructor will teach, but what the learner will achieve. Writing good objectives is a discipline. This session covers: the anatomy of a well-formed learning objective (active verb + observable behavior + condition if required), Bloom's taxonomy as the framework for specifying the cognitive level of an objective (remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, create), and the practical distinction between three training levels that correspond to different Bloom levels: awareness training (participants can recognize and recall — appropriate for L1 courses and for orientation content), skills training (participants can perform a procedure or apply a method — appropriate for L2 courses and for most operator training), and judgment training (participants can analyze, evaluate, and make decisions in novel situations — the target of L3 courses). The session works through examples from the Clark Courses catalog: revising poorly-written objectives, identifying the Bloom level of existing objectives, and drafting objectives for a hypothetical new session. The practical takeaway: the objective dictates the method — you cannot teach judgment by delivering a lecture, and you cannot assess recall by running an open-ended discussion.

**Session 2.2: Session Architecture — Designing a Coherent Training Session**

A well-designed training session has a beginning, a middle, and an end, and each serves a distinct function. This session covers the elements of the session architecture framework used in Clark Courses: the agenda design (what are the three to four main things participants will be able to do by the end, and in what sequence should they be developed?), the warm-up (a brief activity that activates prior knowledge, establishes psychological safety, and calibrates the instructor on the room — not an icebreaker game, but a focused, task-relevant opener), the content delivery sequence (build from familiar to unfamiliar, from concrete to abstract, from simple to complex), the discussion design (what question will generate productive discussion at the right moment? how do you facilitate without controlling?), the consolidation activity (a brief individual or paired task that requires participants to apply or integrate the content just delivered — this is where learning is locked in), and the close (summarize what was learned, connect it forward, and give participants a task to take away). Time management receives specific attention: the most common failure mode in technical instruction is running behind on early modules and compressing or skipping the consolidation and close, which are paradoxically the most important parts for retention.

### Module 3: Instructor Preparation and Delivery

**Session 3.1: Preparing on Technical Content You Haven't Directly Practiced**

This session addresses the anxiety that many intelligent, capable people feel when asked to teach something they haven't done themselves. The professional briefing model is the central concept: a professional briefing officer can provide a thorough, accurate, and useful briefing on a complex technical topic without having personally executed the operation, because briefing is a different skill from operating — it requires understanding the process deeply, representing it clearly, and knowing which details matter for the audience. The preparation pathway for Clark Courses instructors who do not have direct floor experience in a given topic: (1) read the primary sources (the relevant IPC standards, the equipment manufacturer's application notes, the regulatory framework documents) until the content makes sense end-to-end; (2) interview a practitioner with floor experience and build a list of "what does this look like in practice?" questions before the interview; (3) build a robust question set — the five most likely difficult questions on this topic, with prepared answers and with clear notation of where the answer is uncertain; (4) know your knowledge boundary and have a credible, professional protocol for handling questions at the edge of it. The session includes a discussion of how to say "I don't know" in a way that is honest, professional, and preserves rather than undermines credibility.

**Session 3.2: Delivery Mechanics and the Use of Teaching Aids**

Technical content delivery has specific mechanics that are different from general public speaking. This session covers: managing the whiteboard or flip chart as a live thinking tool rather than a presentation surface (drawing a diagram while explaining it is more cognitively engaging for participants than showing a pre-drawn slide), using physical components and boards as teaching props (holding up a BGA while explaining head-in-pillow, showing a conformally coated board while discussing coverage criteria), the role of video in technical delivery (a sixty-second process video of a reflow oven or a SWD programmer in action can anchor hours of verbal explanation), managing a projector or display for reference material (when to project and when to put the projector away and talk), and the use of IPC standards documents as shared reference in the room. The session also covers voice projection and pacing (technical delivery too often becomes a rapid information stream; deliberate pauses after key concepts allow participants to process), managing body language during participant questions, and the difference between answering a question and using a question to deepen the group's learning.

**Session 3.3: Managing the Room — Questions, Digressions, and Difficult Dynamics**

Experienced technical audiences generate challenging instructional situations. This session covers the most common: the participant who knows more than the instructor on a specific subtopic (how to engage their knowledge productively without losing room control), the participant who dominates discussion (how to redirect gracefully), the participant who is silent but whose engagement is uncertain (how to invite participation without singling out), the off-topic question (how to acknowledge and defer), the question the instructor genuinely cannot answer (the "I don't know" protocol from Session 3.1, applied live), and the participant who actively disagrees with course content (distinguish factual disagreement, which requires follow-up with primary sources, from preference disagreement, which should be named as such). The session also covers the regulatory-content caution from CC-L3-03: when a participant asks a question that implies legal interpretation (e.g., "Does our facility need to register under CGP?"), the correct response is clear referral to legal and regulatory counsel, not an answer.

### Module 4: Assessment, Feedback, and Cohort Learning

**Session 4.1: Formative and Summative Assessment — Designing for Learning, Not Just Grading**

Assessment in professional training has two functions that are often confused: formative assessment (checking understanding during the learning experience to adjust delivery and identify gaps — this is for the instructor and the learner, not for a grade) and summative assessment (evaluating achievement at the end of the learning experience against a defined standard — this is for certification, qualification records, and reporting). This session covers: designing formative assessment questions that reveal understanding rather than recall (the difference between "What is the definition of head-in-pillow?" and "Here is an X-ray image — what is the evidence for or against head-in-pillow in this joint, and what would you do next?"), designing summative assessments that are fit-for-purpose (written exams for knowledge-level objectives; performance demonstrations for skills-level objectives; case analyses for judgment-level objectives), the IPC certification framework as a model of summative assessment in electronics manufacturing training, and managing the tension between assessment rigor and adult learner dignity (participants who feel judged rather than supported shut down — the assessment environment must be designed to be challenging but not threatening).

**Session 4.2: Feedback — Delivering It and Receiving It**

Giving constructive feedback to adult learners is a skill that requires both technical command and interpersonal judgment. This session covers the principles of effective feedback in manufacturing training: feedback must be specific (not "that was good" but "your explanation of the paste volume criticality for BGA was clear and well-paced"), grounded in observable behavior rather than character or attitude, timely (as close to the observed performance as possible), and focused on the learnable gap (what specific change would improve performance?). The SBI model (Situation-Behavior-Impact) is introduced as a practical structure for delivering feedback. The session also addresses receiving feedback: the instructor's own delivery will be observed and evaluated by participants, explicitly or implicitly, throughout every session — how to use that feedback productively and how to distinguish actionable feedback from preference or personality mismatch. Peer feedback during practice deliveries in this course is structured using the SBI model, so participants both give and receive feedback before the session ends.

### Module 5: The Clark Partnership Model and Ecosystem

**Session 5.1: How Clark Courses Are Licensed and Delivered**

Clark Courses are professional educational products developed and quality-assured by Clark. This session covers the Clark Courses licensing and delivery model: the train-the-trainer pathway (Clark delivers the course to prospective instructors; instructors are assessed against delivery standards; qualified instructors are licensed to deliver Clark Courses content within defined scope); the curriculum licensing structure for corporate in-house delivery (an organization licenses the right to deliver specific Clark Courses to their own employees, using Clark-provided materials, under Clark quality standards); and the co-delivery model (a Clark-qualified instructor co-delivers with an organization's subject-matter expert, combining Clark's pedagogical standards with the organization's specific operational context). The quality standards for licensed delivery are covered: instructor qualification documentation, material currency (licensed materials must be kept current with the Clark Courses edition in use), cohort reporting (attendance, assessment results, and feedback submitted to Clark for quality monitoring), and the renewal cycle for instructor qualification.

**Session 5.2: IPC Certification Pathways and Clark Courses Alignment**

Clark Courses are designed to be intellectually compatible with and preparatory to IPC certification pathways. This session maps the alignment: CC-L1-03 and CC-L1-04 build the foundation for IPC-A-610 CIS preparation; CC-L2-02 builds toward J-STD-001 CIS preparation; CC-L3-01 (Class 3 Workmanship) covers the core CIS body of knowledge at an advanced level. The session covers the IPC certification ecosystem: the difference between CIS (Certified IPC Specialist — the inspector/operator qualification) and CIT (Certified IPC Trainer — the trainer qualification), the EPTAC partnership with IPC for training and examination delivery, and how the Clark-EPTAC relationship provides a pathway from Clark Courses to formal IPC certification. Participants discuss how to use Clark Courses and IPC certification together in an organizational training plan: Clark Courses for broad manufacturing knowledge and conceptual depth; IPC certification for the formal, auditable qualification record that quality systems require for Class 3 and regulated work.

**Session 5.3: The Clark Ecosystem — Hamilton, IPE, and the Node Network**

This session situates Clark Courses within the broader Clark mission and operational model. The Clark Hamilton facility: a working electronics manufacturing operation that serves as both a production environment and a teaching environment — products built at Hamilton are built to the same standards taught in Clark Courses, making the facility a living demonstration of the curriculum. The Clark Integrated Production Environment (IPE): the digital platform that runs at Hamilton and at Clark nodes, connecting manufacturing data (component traceability, process records, firmware provenance, inspection results) into a unified unit history — the operational backbone of the quality system, and the platform that Clark Courses teach participants to understand and use. The node network: Clark's model for extending its manufacturing and educational capabilities to partner facilities — each node operates under Clark standards and uses the Clark IPE platform, creating a consistent quality baseline across the network. Clark Courses instructors who understand the ecosystem can position training content not as abstract knowledge but as the operating philosophy of a real, running manufacturing system, which is a fundamentally more compelling and credible framing.

## 7. Key Terms and Concepts

**Andragogy** — The theory and practice of adult learning, developed by Malcolm Knowles; distinguished from pedagogy by its emphasis on self-direction, experience as a learning resource, problem-orientation, and intrinsic motivation.

**Learning Objective** — A precise statement of what a learner will be able to do at the end of a learning experience; written with an active verb specifying the cognitive or behavioral level and an observable, measurable outcome.

**Bloom's Taxonomy** — A hierarchical framework for classifying cognitive learning objectives, from lower-order (remember, understand, apply) to higher-order (analyze, evaluate, create); used to design instruction and assessment at the appropriate cognitive level.

**Formative Assessment** — Assessment conducted during the learning process to provide feedback that guides further learning; not summative; examples include questions, exit tickets, practice exercises, and group discussion.

**Summative Assessment** — Assessment conducted at the end of a learning experience to evaluate achievement against a defined standard; examples include written exams, performance demonstrations, and IPC certification examinations.

**Train-the-Trainer (T3)** — A training delivery model in which a master instructor teaches prospective instructors the content and delivery methods of a course, qualifying them to deliver the same content independently to subsequent cohorts.

**Professional Briefing Model** — The approach to instructor preparation in which the instructor develops sufficient conceptual command of a technical subject through primary source reading, practitioner interviews, and question set development, enabling credible delivery without personal shop-floor experience in the subject.

**Warm-Up** — A brief, focused activity at the start of a training session designed to activate prior knowledge, establish psychological safety, and calibrate the instructor on the range of experience in the room; not an icebreaker but a task-relevant cognitive primer.

**Consolidation Activity** — A brief individual or paired task following content delivery that requires participants to apply or integrate what they have just learned; the mechanism by which new knowledge is converted from short-term to long-term memory.

**SBI Feedback Model** — Situation-Behavior-Impact: a structured approach to delivering feedback that anchors the observation in a specific situation, describes the observable behavior (not the person), and explains the impact of that behavior on the learning or outcome.

**Clark Courses License** — The commercial agreement under which an organization is authorized to deliver specific Clark Courses content to its own employees, under Clark quality standards, using Clark-provided materials.

**CIS (Certified IPC Specialist)** — The IPC certification for inspectors and operators who have demonstrated competency against IPC-A-610 and/or J-STD-001 workmanship standards; the standard inspector qualification credential in regulated electronics manufacturing.

**EPTAC** — IPC's primary authorized training partner for IPC certification programs in North America; administers training and examinations for CIS, CIT, and other IPC credentials.

**Clark IPE (Integrated Production Environment)** — Clark's digital platform for managing production data across the full unit lifecycle: component traceability, process records, firmware provenance, inspection results, and quality reporting; the operational backbone of Clark's quality system and the platform taught in Clark Courses.

**Cohort Reporting** — The reporting obligation under Clark Courses licensing in which the delivering instructor submits attendance, assessment results, and participant feedback to Clark after each course delivery; supports curriculum quality monitoring and instructor qualification maintenance.

## 8. Instructor Notes

**On this course being its own model:** CC-L3-05 is unusual in the Clark Courses catalog because it teaches instructional practice by demonstrating it. The instructor delivering this course is in a teaching-about-teaching situation, and every delivery choice they make is visible as a model or anti-model. The instructor should be deliberately and explicitly meta about this: "Notice that I opened with a warm-up question before the first agenda item — that is the technique we will discuss in Module 2." This meta-commentary does not undermine the teaching; it deepens it.

**On the practice delivery sessions:** The two practice deliveries in Module 3 are the most important experiential elements in the course. Participants should be assigned topics in advance (a five-to-ten minute technical explanation from the Clark Courses catalog, something like "explain the head-in-pillow failure mechanism" or "explain why process validation is required under ISO 13485"), given fifteen minutes of preparation time, and then deliver to the group. The feedback session after each delivery should use the SBI model explicitly. The instructor should deliver feedback first, then invite peer feedback, and then invite the participant's own self-reflection. Timekeeping during feedback is important — five minutes per delivery is a reasonable target.

**On the "you don't need floor experience" argument:** Session 3.1 makes an argument that may be provocative for some participants: that it is possible to deliver credible, high-quality technical training on content you haven't personally practiced. The instructor should be prepared for pushback from experienced practitioners who are skeptical of this claim. The response is not to defend the claim in the abstract but to demonstrate it in the room — the instructor's own delivery of the Clark Courses material (which covers topics like BGA rework and Class 3 process control that not all instructors will have directly practiced) is the evidence. The key is the distinction between performing a skill and understanding and representing it clearly. A good briefing officer does not pretend to have flown the mission; they make sure the pilot has the information they need to fly it well.

**On the Clark ecosystem content:** Session 5.3 covers Clark's Hamilton facility, IPE platform, and node network. The instructor should ensure they have current, accurate information about these before delivery — this is operational content that can become outdated and must reflect the actual state of the Clark business at the time of delivery. The instructor should coordinate with Clark's operations team before the course to verify current status.

**On participant diversity:** Participants in this course may range from very experienced senior engineers taking a reflective approach to their own existing training practice, to people who are entirely new to training delivery and feel genuinely anxious about the practice delivery sessions. The instructor must manage this range carefully. The experienced participants can be invited to share practice and reflect critically on it; the newer participants need psychological safety and a clear framework. Setting the right tone in the first thirty minutes — that this is a learning space, that practice deliveries are expected to be imperfect, that feedback is developmental rather than evaluative — is critical.

## 9. Hands-On and Discussion Exercises

**Exercise 1: Learning Objective Rewriting**

Participants receive a set of ten poorly-written learning objectives drawn from a hypothetical manufacturing training program: some are too vague ("understand soldering"), some are written as instructor actions ("explain how reflow works") rather than learner outcomes, some conflate objectives at multiple Bloom levels, and some describe activities rather than outcomes. Working in pairs, participants rewrite each objective to be specific, measurable, written in active verb format, and at the appropriate Bloom level for the intended audience. Pairs compare their rewrites and the instructor facilitates a discussion of the most contested cases. The debrief connects the exercise to course design: once you have well-formed objectives, the assessment writes itself — you simply test whether the participant can do what the objective says.

**Exercise 2: Practice Delivery and SBI Feedback**

Each participant prepares a five-to-ten minute technical delivery on an assigned topic from the Clark Courses catalog. After each delivery, the delivering participant receives: (a) feedback from the instructor using the SBI model (two specific strengths, one specific development area), and (b) structured peer feedback using a provided feedback form (one thing that helped you understand the content; one question you still have; one thing that would have made it easier to follow). The group then discusses: What is the difference between feedback that is genuinely useful and feedback that is just opinion? How does the SBI structure help? This exercise is the experiential core of the course — participants remember it long after the conceptual content has faded.

**Exercise 3: Training Plan Design**

Participants receive a scenario: an electronics manufacturer has decided to pursue ISO 13485 certification and needs to develop a training program that will qualify all production operators and inspectors on the quality system requirements, soldering standards, and conformal coating procedures relevant to medical device assembly. Working in small groups, participants design a training plan: identify the learning objectives, map them to Clark Courses content (or other sources), sequence the training for a new hire and for an experienced employee being re-qualified, determine what assessment is required for each course element, and identify the records that must be generated and retained for the quality system. Groups present their plans and the instructor facilitates a discussion of: What did different groups prioritize? What did the quality system requirements constrain? What assumptions had to be made about the workforce? This exercise integrates the andragogy content, the curriculum design content, and the regulatory compliance content from CC-L3-03 in a single applied task.

## 10. Assessment Suggestions

**Observed Practice Delivery (Primary Assessment):** Each participant delivers one complete five-to-ten minute technical session segment on an assigned topic. The instructor assesses against a rubric covering: objective clarity (can the assessor infer a clear learning objective from the delivery?), content accuracy (is the technical content correct?), structure (does the delivery have a clear beginning, middle, and end?), participant engagement (does the instructor engage the room or deliver at it?), and handling of one deliberate "difficult question" introduced by the instructor during the delivery. This is the most valid assessment for a course about instructional practice — the proof is in the delivery.

**Session Design Artifact:** Participants produce a complete design document for one training session of their choosing (from the Clark Courses catalog or from their own organization's training needs). The document includes: stated learning objectives at the correct Bloom level, a timed agenda, a warm-up activity, the content sequence rationale, one consolidation activity design, and the summative assessment. Evaluated on completeness, internal consistency (does the assessment match the objectives?), and practical viability (could an instructor who was not the designer deliver this session?).

**Reflective Written Response:** After the practice delivery and feedback session, participants write a one-page reflection: What did the feedback tell me about my delivery? What will I do differently in my next delivery? What aspect of instructional practice do I most want to develop, and how will I develop it? This is evaluated for depth of reflection and specificity of development plan, not for a "correct" answer.
