# CC-L3-03: Regulatory and Compliance — ITAR, Controlled Goods, ISO 13485, AS9100, and IEC 60601

**Clark Courses — Level 3: Advanced Topics**
**Facility:** Clark, Hamilton, Ontario
**Course Code:** CC-L3-03

---

## 1. Course Overview

Regulatory compliance is not paperwork for its own sake — it is the legal and operational framework that determines which markets a manufacturer can serve, which customers will award contracts, and which employees can see which information. This course provides manufacturing professionals with a serious, practical grounding in the major regulatory frameworks that govern defense, aerospace, and medical electronics manufacturing: US export controls under ITAR, Canada's Controlled Goods Program and Export Control List, quality management standards for medical devices (ISO 13485) and aerospace (AS9100 Rev D), the IEC 60601 electrical safety framework for medical electrical equipment, and the NADCAP accreditation program. The course treats compliance not as a legal checkbox but as a set of operational disciplines — process validation, change control, document management, non-conformance handling — that make a manufacturing operation genuinely more capable, not just more auditable. Participants who complete this course will be able to engage knowledgeably with compliance requirements, contribute to audit readiness, and understand the criminal and commercial consequences of non-compliance.

---

## 2. Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, participants will be able to:

- Explain the scope and key provisions of ITAR, identify the categories of defense articles and services covered by the US Munitions List (USML), and describe the licensing and compliance obligations that apply to a Canadian EMS company performing work for a US defense customer
- Describe Canada's Controlled Goods Program (CGP) registration requirements, the security assessment process, and the record-keeping obligations, and explain how CGP and ITAR interact for a Canadian facility handling US-origin controlled technical data
- Distinguish ISO 13485 from ISO 9001 in terms of design controls, process validation requirements, complaint handling, and CAPA, and identify what a contract electronics manufacturer must implement to support a medical device OEM's quality system
- Describe the structure and scope of AS9100 Rev D, identify the aviation-specific additions beyond ISO 9001 (risk management, configuration management, FOD prevention, key characteristics, first article inspection), and explain what FAIR per AS9102 must contain
- Explain the purpose and structure of IEC 60601, the concept of essential performance, and how the standard interacts with FDA 510(k) clearance, CE marking under the EU MDR, and Health Canada licensing for medical electrical equipment
- Describe the NADCAP accreditation model for electronics/electrical processes and explain how NADCAP fits into an AS9100 quality system at an aerospace supplier
- Apply core cross-cutting compliance disciplines — document control, change management, non-conformance management, internal audit, and supplier qualification — to a manufacturing scenario

---

## 3. Target Audience

This course is aimed at quality managers, quality engineers, manufacturing engineers, operations managers, and business development professionals at EMS companies, OEMs, and contract manufacturers who serve defense, aerospace, or medical customers. It is also appropriate for regulatory affairs staff at medical device companies who need to understand how their manufacturing partners' quality systems interact with their own. Program managers and senior technical staff who negotiate customer contracts will benefit from understanding what regulatory compliance clauses in contracts actually require of the operation.

**Duration:** 2 days (approximately 14 instructional hours)

This course has a higher proportion of policy and procedure content than other L3 courses; keeping it grounded with real scenarios is essential to avoiding abstraction.

## 6. Module Outline

### Module 1: Export Controls — ITAR and the US Munitions List

**Session 1.1: What ITAR Is and Why It Matters for a Canadian EMS Shop**

The International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) is a set of US government regulations that control the export and import of defense-related articles and services listed on the US Munitions List (USML). The key point for a Canadian manufacturer: ITAR is a US law with extra-territorial reach. If a Canadian company receives US-origin technical data, defense articles, or manufacturing services for items on the USML, that Canadian company is subject to ITAR compliance obligations — and violations carry criminal penalties under the US Arms Export Control Act, including fines of up to $1 million per violation and imprisonment. This session covers: the ITAR regulatory framework (22 CFR 120-130), the structure of the USML (Categories I through XXI, with emphasis on electronics-relevant categories: XV Space and Airborne Systems, XI Military Electronics), the definition of "defense article" and "defense service," the license requirement and exemption framework (particularly the Canadian exemptions under the US-Canada Defense Production Sharing Agreement), and what triggers ITAR obligations for a Canadian EMS company.

**Session 1.2: Practical ITAR Compliance in a Manufacturing Environment**

ITAR compliance in manufacturing is not abstract — it has specific operational implications that affect how a facility is organized, how information flows, and who can be in which rooms. This session covers: the "US person" vs. "foreign person" distinction under ITAR and its implications for employee access to controlled technical data; what constitutes a "deemed export" (showing controlled technical data to a foreign national, even on Canadian soil, may constitute an export requiring a license); data handling requirements for ITAR-controlled drawings and specifications (access controls, storage, prohibition on emailing to unauthorized parties); physical security requirements for a facility handling ITAR-controlled hardware (segregation, access logs); and the ITAR registration requirement for US manufacturers and brokers (Canadian companies are typically not required to register with DDTC directly, but must ensure their US customer's license or exemption covers the transaction). The session closes with a discussion of the consequences of non-compliance: documented cases of criminal prosecution and large civil penalties.

---

### Module 2: Canada's Controlled Goods Program and Export Control List

**Session 2.1: The Controlled Goods Program (CGP) — Registration, Security, and Records**

Canada's Controlled Goods Program (CGP), administered under the Defence Production Act, regulates the examination, possession, and transfer of controlled goods within Canada. Unlike ITAR (which is about export), CGP applies within Canada: a Canadian company that examines, possesses, or transfers controlled goods (military equipment, technical data, and items that could pose a national security risk) must be registered with Public Services and Procurement Canada (PSPC). This session covers: which goods are controlled (Schedule to the Defence Production Act, which maps closely to but is not identical to the ITAR USML), the registration process for organizations (designated official, security assessment, site inspection), the personal security assessment (PSA) for individuals who handle controlled goods, the record-keeping requirements (who handled controlled goods, when, under what authorization), and the penalties for non-compliance (administrative penalties, revocation of registration, criminal prosecution under the Defence Production Act).

**Session 2.2: CGP and ITAR Interaction — Managing Both Regimes**

A Canadian EMS company doing work for a US defense OEM on hardware that is ITAR-controlled may simultaneously be subject to both CGP (because the hardware is controlled in Canada) and ITAR (because US-origin technical data or defense articles are involved). This session maps the interaction: where CGP and ITAR overlap (similar controlled item scope, similar access control requirements), where they diverge (CGP has broader Canadian-origin controlled goods scope; ITAR has license/exemption framework and deemed export provisions that CGP does not), and how a practical compliance program manages both. The session also introduces the Canadian Export Control List (ECL) — Canada's export control regime for dual-use and military goods — and explains how it differs from CGP (ECL governs what leaves Canada; CGP governs what is handled within Canada).

---

### Module 3: Medical Device Compliance — ISO 13485, IEC 60601, and Regulatory Pathways

**Session 3.1: ISO 13485 — Medical Device QMS and How It Differs from ISO 9001**

ISO 13485 is the quality management system standard for organizations in the medical device supply chain. It is built on the ISO 9001 framework but has significant additions that reflect the life-safety nature of medical devices. This session covers the key differences: design controls (ISO 13485 has far more prescriptive requirements for design and development planning, input/output, verification/validation, and transfer to production); process validation (processes whose output cannot be fully verified by inspection must be validated — this is a much stronger requirement than ISO 9001); product-specific requirements (sterile devices, software, implantable devices); complaint handling and post-market surveillance; CAPA (corrective and preventive action — the engine of the QMS); and the risk management integration requirement (ISO 14971 risk management must be integrated throughout the product lifecycle). For an EMS company serving a medical OEM: the EMS company's QMS must be compatible with and supportable of the OEM's design control and product record requirements — this has specific implications for document control, traveler design, and change management.

**Session 3.2: IEC 60601 — Electrical Safety and Essential Performance for Medical Electrical Equipment**

IEC 60601-1 (Medical Electrical Equipment — General Requirements for Basic Safety and Essential Performance) is the global electrical safety standard for medical electrical equipment. It defines "basic safety" (protection against mechanical, electrical, thermal, radiation, and chemical hazards) and "essential performance" (performance necessary to avoid unacceptable risk — the machine must not only be safe; it must also work). This session covers: the structure of the IEC 60601 family (collateral standards for particular risks — electromagnetic compatibility: 60601-1-2, programmable electrical medical systems: 60601-1-4 — and particular standards for specific device types — 60601-2-x), the concept of essential performance and its interaction with design controls, the test requirements that affect manufacturing (dielectric strength, earth continuity, leakage current — tests that may be performed on every unit at end-of-line), and the edition history (3rd edition is current; some markets accepted 2nd edition longer than others).

**Session 3.3: FDA, CE Marking, Health Canada — Regulatory Pathways for Medical Devices**

Medical device manufacturers must navigate market-specific regulatory approval pathways before commercial distribution. This session provides an overview of the three most relevant to a Canadian manufacturer: FDA (510(k) substantial equivalence clearance for Class II devices, PMA for Class III, registration and listing requirements for manufacturers including contract manufacturers), CE marking under the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745, which replaced the MDD in 2021 — now requires notified body review for Class IIa and above, clinical evidence requirements, and post-market surveillance), and Health Canada device licensing under the Medical Devices Regulations (Class I self-declaration, Class II-IV licence application, establishment licence for manufacturers and importers). The key point for an EMS company: serving a medical OEM requires that the EMS company's QMS and manufacturing records be capable of supporting the OEM's regulatory submissions — this is a commercial qualification requirement, not just a quality preference.

### Module 4: Aerospace and Defense Quality — AS9100 and NADCAP

**Session 4.1: AS9100 Rev D — The Aerospace Addition Set**

AS9100 Rev D is the aerospace quality management system standard: it takes ISO 9001:2015 as its base and adds a substantial set of aerospace-specific requirements. This session covers the most significant additions for an electronics manufacturer: risk management (AS9100 formalizes risk identification, assessment, and mitigation planning throughout the QMS, with specific treatment of program-level risk), configuration management (the system for managing the design baseline and ensuring that what is being manufactured always matches the current approved design), FOD (Foreign Object Damage/Debris) prevention program requirements (formal program with controls, training, and records), first article inspection (AS9100 requires FAI for new products and after significant changes), key characteristics (identifying and controlling the product and process characteristics that most affect fit, form, and function), and customer-specific requirements (the aerospace primes — Boeing, Lockheed, Airbus, Raytheon — all have CS requirements that supplement AS9100). The session also covers the AS9100 certification audit process and the IAQG (International Aerospace Quality Group) OASIS database, where certification status is publicly registered.

**Session 4.2: NADCAP — Process Accreditation for Aerospace**

NADCAP (National Aerospace and Defense Contractors Accreditation Program) is a cooperative industry program administered by PRI (Performance Review Institute) that provides process-specific accreditation for special processes in aerospace and defense. Unlike AS9100 (which certifies the quality management system), NADCAP accredits specific processes — the audit verifies that a specific process is being performed correctly, with qualified equipment, qualified personnel, and adequate process controls. The electronics/electrical (E&E) NADCAP accreditation is the relevant one for PCB assembly: it covers soldering processes, wiring harness assembly, and conformal coating. This session covers: which aerospace customers require NADCAP accreditation of their EMS suppliers, what an E&E NADCAP audit covers (process procedures, equipment calibration, operator qualification, nonconformance management, specific process controls), the audit cycle and ongoing maintenance, and how NADCAP accreditation interacts with and complements AS9100 certification.

**Session 4.3: First Article Inspection Report (FAIR) per AS9102**

AS9102 is the standard specifying the requirements for First Article Inspection Reports in aerospace. A FAIR is not an inspection report in the conventional sense — it is a documented verification that the first production article (or first article after a significant change) conforms to all engineering documentation requirements, and that the manufacturing process is capable of producing conforming product. This session covers: the AS9102 FAIR structure (eight defined sections: design documentation, product accounting, product characteristics, material and process verification, dimensional verification, functional verification, appearance, and customer-specific requirements), what "substantial conformance" means in the FAIR context, the difference between a FAIR and a First Piece Inspection (the FAIR is a formal contractual deliverable; the first piece inspection is an internal process control step), and how to manage a FAIR when some characteristics require long-lead testing (thermal cycling, vibration, accelerated aging) that cannot be completed before shipment of the first article.

### Module 5: Cross-Cutting Compliance Disciplines

**Session 5.1: Document Control, Change Management, and Record Retention**

Every compliance framework covered in this course — ISO 13485, AS9100, ITAR, CGP — imposes document control and record retention requirements. This session covers the common infrastructure: the document control system (document identification, revision control, approval chain, distribution control, obsolescence management), the change management process (engineering change orders, change impact assessment, re-validation requirements after changes, customer notification obligations for changes on controlled items), and record retention (retention periods vary by standard and contract — medical device records may need to be retained for the expected useful life of the device plus a defined period; ITAR records must be retained for five years from the end of the transaction; AS9100 records are contract-specific but typically seven to ten years is the floor for aerospace). The session addresses the practical challenge of multi-framework document control: a single procedure document may need to satisfy requirements from ISO 13485, AS9100, and a customer-specific quality plan simultaneously.

**Session 5.2: Non-Conformance Management, Internal Audit, and Supplier Qualification**

Non-conformance management is the mechanism by which a quality system captures, contains, and corrects departures from specification — and generates the data used to drive systemic improvement. This session covers: the non-conformance record (NCR) and material review board (MRB) process, the disposition options (use-as-is, rework, repair, scrap, return to supplier), the CAPA (corrective and preventive action) process and its role in closing the loop between non-conformances and process improvement, and the metrics that should be tracked (NCR rate by product, by operation, by supplier, by defect type). Internal audit is covered as the systematic process for verifying that the QMS is operating as designed and that compliance requirements are being met — audit planning, conducting objective-evidence-based audits, writing effective findings, and closing findings with verified corrective actions. Supplier qualification and ongoing supplier management rounds out the session: approved vendor list (AVL) management, supplier audit requirements under ISO 13485 and AS9100, the specific requirements for qualifying a new supplier for controlled work (ITAR-registered suppliers for defense work, CGP-registered suppliers for Canadian controlled goods), and supplier performance monitoring.

## 7. Key Terms and Concepts

**ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations)** — US regulations (22 CFR Parts 120-130) governing the export and import of defense articles and services listed on the US Munitions List; violations carry criminal penalties with extra-territorial reach.

**USML (US Munitions List)** — The list of defense articles and services controlled under ITAR, organized into 21 categories; items on the USML cannot be exported without a license or applicable exemption.

**Deemed Export** — Under ITAR, the disclosure of controlled technical data to a foreign national within the United States (or, by extension, in Canada by analogy) may constitute an export requiring a license, even when no physical article crosses a border.

**Controlled Goods Program (CGP)** — Canada's regulatory program (under the Defence Production Act) governing the examination, possession, and transfer of controlled goods within Canada; requires organizational registration and personal security assessments for individuals with access.

**ISO 13485** — The international quality management system standard specific to organizations in the medical device supply chain; requires design controls, process validation, and CAPA at a higher level of prescription than ISO 9001.

**Process Validation (IQ/OQ/PQ)** — The documented procedure for establishing evidence that a manufacturing process consistently produces a product meeting its specification; IQ (Installation Qualification) verifies equipment is installed correctly; OQ (Operational Qualification) verifies it operates correctly across its range; PQ (Performance Qualification) verifies it produces acceptable product under production conditions.

**IEC 60601** — The international standard for safety and essential performance of medical electrical equipment; the 1-1 general standard is supplemented by collateral and particular standards for specific risks and device types.

**Essential Performance** — Under IEC 60601, performance of a clinical function that, if lost, would result in unacceptable risk; loss of essential performance must be treated with the same severity as a physical safety hazard.

**AS9100** — The aerospace quality management system standard, built on ISO 9001:2015 with aviation, space, and defense additions including risk management, configuration management, FOD prevention, and first article inspection requirements.

**NADCAP** — National Aerospace and Defense Contractors Accreditation Program; provides process-specific accreditation audits for special processes (including electronics assembly) accepted by major aerospace prime contractors as an alternative to individual prime audits.

**FAIR (First Article Inspection Report)** — A documented verification, per AS9102, that the first production article conforms to all engineering documentation requirements and that the manufacturing process is capable of producing conforming product; a formal contractual deliverable in aerospace.

**Key Characteristic** — A product or process characteristic whose variation has a significant effect on product fit, form, function, performance, service life, or manufacturability; identified through design analysis and subject to tighter control and measurement in production.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)** — A formal quality system process for investigating the root cause of non-conformances (corrective) and for eliminating the potential causes of non-conformances before they occur (preventive); mandated by ISO 13485 and AS9100.

**Configuration Management** — The discipline of identifying, documenting, and controlling the functional and physical characteristics of a product at defined points in its lifecycle; ensures that what is being manufactured matches the current approved design.

**510(k)** — The premarket notification pathway under US FDA regulations for medical devices that are substantially equivalent to a legally marketed predicate device; required for most Class II medical devices before commercial distribution in the United States.

## 8. Instructor Notes

**On legal content:** This course covers regulatory frameworks that have legal and criminal dimensions. The instructor should be clear that the course provides general education and awareness — it is not legal advice. Participants with specific compliance questions about their organization's obligations should consult legal counsel and the relevant regulatory authority directly. This caveat should be stated explicitly at the start of the module covering ITAR and at any point where the discussion approaches specific compliance obligations.

**On ITAR for Canadian audiences:** ITAR is a US law. Canadian manufacturers are sometimes surprised to learn that it can apply to them. The session on ITAR practical compliance should spend real time on the mechanism of obligation: the US customer's license or agreement authorizes the Canadian company to receive controlled technical data or articles, and in accepting that authorization, the Canadian company takes on compliance obligations. The instructor should have read the US-Canada Defense Production Sharing Agreement (DPSA) and the relevant ITAR exemptions (22 CFR 126.5, 126.6) before delivering this module.

**On ISO 13485 and QMS scope:** Many EMS companies have ISO 9001 but not ISO 13485. The transition to medical work often requires upgrading the QMS. The instructor should be candid about the scale of that undertaking — it is not simply a certification addition; it typically requires new procedures for design controls (even for a contract manufacturer that is not the device designer), process validation, and complaint handling. Underestimating this scope is a common source of problems when EMS companies take on medical work.

**On NADCAP:** The instructor should note that NADCAP accreditation is not universally required for all aerospace electronics work — it depends on the customer and the contract. Some prime contractors require it; others accept AS9100 certification with process-specific evidence. The instructor should verify the current state of NADCAP adoption for electronics/electrical processes in Canada before delivery, as the landscape evolves.

**On the "compliance as capability" framing:** This course is designed to resist the common "compliance as burden" framing. The instructor should consistently return to the argument that the disciplines required by these frameworks — document control, process validation, non-conformance management, internal audit — make a manufacturing operation more capable and more profitable, not just more auditable. The most successful regulated manufacturers do not experience compliance as overhead; they experience it as the operating system of a well-run business.

**Instructor background recommendation:** This course benefits significantly from an instructor who has worked in a regulated environment — medical device, aerospace, or defense. Regulatory compliance knowledge that comes entirely from reading standards, without the experience of living inside a regulated quality system during an audit or during a customer qualification, will lack the texture and judgment that participants in regulated industries will notice immediately.

## 9. Hands-On and Discussion Exercises

**Exercise 1: ITAR Flow-Down Scenario Analysis**

Participants receive a hypothetical contract scenario: a Canadian EMS company has been approached by a US defense OEM to assemble a printed circuit board assembly used in an airborne electronic warfare system (USML Category XI). The OEM will provide Gerber files, BOMs, and test software. The EMS company will source components, manage process materials, and ship finished assemblies. Working in small groups, participants identify: Is this item on the USML? What license or exemption does the transaction require? What access controls must the EMS company implement for the technical data? Which employees can work on this program, and what screening is required? What records must be kept, and for how long? Groups present their analysis and the instructor provides a structured debrief, highlighting the most common compliance gaps in this scenario type.

**Exercise 2: ISO 13485 Gap Assessment**

Participants receive a hypothetical process description for an EMS company with ISO 9001 certification that is onboarding its first medical device customer. The process description is accurate for ISO 9001 but has been deliberately written without ISO 13485 additions. Working individually or in pairs, participants identify: What is missing for ISO 13485 compliance? Where are the specific gaps in design controls, process validation, complaint handling, and CAPA? Which gaps are documentation gaps (procedures exist but aren't documented correctly) and which are systematic gaps (the process simply doesn't exist yet)? Participants draft a prioritized gap remediation list. The debrief discusses which gaps are critical (must be closed before first production run) and which can be closed on a longer timeline.

**Exercise 3: FAIR Section Review**

Participants receive a set of fabricated AS9102 FAIR documents for a hypothetical PCB assembly. One FAIR is complete and correct; one is missing required sections; one has sections present but with inadequate objective evidence. Working against the AS9102 section requirements (provided as a reference sheet), participants evaluate each FAIR: Which sections are missing? Where is the evidence insufficient? What additional evidence would satisfy the requirement? This exercise builds practical familiarity with the FAIR structure and the difference between a FAIR that will be accepted by an aerospace customer and one that will be returned for rework.

## 10. Assessment Suggestions

**Regulatory Framework Mapping Quiz (15-20 questions):** Short-answer and matching questions covering which requirements come from which frameworks (ITAR vs. CGP vs. ISO 13485 vs. AS9100), the key thresholds and obligations in each, and the interaction points between frameworks. Tests knowledge retention of a content-heavy course.

**Compliance Scenario Written Response:** Participants receive a scenario (e.g., a new customer RFQ for a medical device assembly; a potential ITAR-controlled defense contract; an AS9100 internal audit finding) and write a structured response identifying the applicable requirements, the gaps in the current operation, and a remediation plan. This format assesses analytical application of the regulatory knowledge.

**Mock Internal Audit Exercise:** In pairs, one participant acts as auditor and one as auditee for a defined scope (e.g., document control, non-conformance management, or process validation). The auditor conducts a five-to-ten minute audit against a specified standard clause, documents a finding if warranted, and discusses it with the auditee. This exercise develops both audit skill and the experience of being audited effectively.
